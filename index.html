What Is Disciplinary Literacy?

Students learn how people in a field read, write, communicate, investigate, evaluate evidence, and produce knowledge.

Core Idea

Students do not just “read more.” They learn how experts in each discipline make meaning: scientists interpret data and models, historians corroborate sources, mathematicians justify reasoning, and artists critique choices.

Key Themes

Problem drivenAuthentic textsEvidenceDiscussionWriting

Literacy practices help students learn the discipline, not complete a separate activity.

Quick Classroom Test

Ask: “What would a literate person in this field do with information?” Then plan student tasks that mirror that work.

10 Essential Classroom Practices

Filter by role or search for a strategy.

1. Problem- or Inquiry-Based Instruction

Start with an authentic problem, phenomenon, issue, or question that creates a reason to read, write, investigate, and communicate.

2. Collaborative Reasoning and Discussion

Use accountable discussion routines that require students to explain, challenge, clarify, and extend using evidence.

3. Discipline-Specific Reading and Viewing

Teach students how experts approach texts, graphs, models, maps, data, images, simulations, and media.

4. Discipline-Specific Writing

Use writing to explain, justify, argue, model, reflect, prove, recommend, or communicate to an audience.

5. Academic and Discipline-Specific Language

Teach vocabulary, symbols, word parts, and sentence structures in context.

6. Culturally Sustaining and Responsive Literacy

Connect texts, problems, and tasks to students’ cultures, identities, communities, and lived experiences.

7. Evidence Evaluation and Source Use

Help students evaluate what counts as evidence and how evidence supports claims in each discipline.

8. Strategy Instruction and Metacognition

Model expert thinking and help students monitor, reflect, revise, and transfer strategies.

9. Digital, Multimodal, and Media Literacy

Use digital tools, images, simulations, video, databases, and media to deepen content learning.

10. Assessment, Feedback, and Transfer

Use formative evidence to support content growth, strategy use, language development, and transfer.

Subject-Specific Strategies

How disciplinary literacy looks across content areas.

Science

  • Interpret graphs, models, and data tables.
  • Use Claim-Evidence-Reasoning.
  • Analyze experimental design and error.

Mathematics

  • Justify solutions using multiple representations.
  • Read worked examples and proofs.
  • Write explanations of reasoning.

Social Studies

  • Source, contextualize, and corroborate documents.
  • Analyze bias and perspective.
  • Construct evidence-based arguments.

ELA

  • Analyze author’s craft, structure, and purpose.
  • Support interpretations with evidence.
  • Write for audience and purpose.

CTE

  • Read technical manuals, schematics, and procedures.
  • Write reports and recommendations.
  • Use workplace language and tools.

Arts & Electives

  • Critique using discipline-specific language.
  • Interpret visual, musical, or performance texts.
  • Write artist statements and reflections.

Role Dashboards

Classroom Teacher

  • Identify the unit problem or question.
  • Plan texts, discussion, writing, vocabulary, and evidence routines.
  • Use formative evidence to adjust supports.

Building Administrator

  • Build a representative literacy leadership team.
  • Protect collaboration time.
  • Observe student sense-making and provide coaching.

District Leader

  • Align curriculum and professional learning.
  • Support job-embedded implementation.
  • Monitor equity, access, and long-term outcomes.

Planning Tools

Unit Planning Protocol

  1. Anchor the unit with a problem or question.
  2. Identify texts, data, models, or media.
  3. Plan discourse, writing, and evidence use.
  4. Teach language demands explicitly.
  5. Collect student evidence.

PLC Conversation Stems

  • What disciplinary thinking are we asking students to do?
  • What literacy demand may block content learning?
  • What student evidence will we bring back?

Communication Toolkit

Staff Message

Disciplinary literacy is not a separate program. It is how students use reading, writing, speaking, listening, viewing, and evidence-based reasoning to think deeply in every subject area.

Family Message

Students are learning how experts in each subject use information, evidence, and communication.

Equity Check
  • Whose voices and experiences are represented?
  • What background knowledge is assumed?
  • How will students show understanding in more than one way?
