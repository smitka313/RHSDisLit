Start Here: What Is Disciplinary Literacy?

Disciplinary literacy means students learn how people in a field read, write, communicate, investigate, evaluate evidence, and produce knowledge. It looks different in biology, mathematics, history, engineering, art, health, and other subjects.

Core Idea

Students do not just “read more.” They learn how experts in each discipline make meaning: scientists interpret data and models, historians corroborate sources, mathematicians justify reasoning, and artists critique choices.

Key Themes

Problem or question driven Authentic texts Unit-level integration Community connection Language awareness

Use literacy practices because they help students learn the discipline, not because literacy is a separate activity.

Quick Classroom Test

Ask: “What would a literate person in this field do with information?” Then plan student tasks that mirror that work.

  • What texts/data will students use?
  • What evidence will they evaluate?
  • What claim, model, solution, or product will they communicate?

10 Essential Classroom Practices

Filter by role or search for a strategy. These practices are adapted for practical classroom reference across content areas.

1. Problem- or Inquiry-Based Instruction

Use when: launching a unit, anchoring a phenomenon, framing a historical question, presenting a design challenge, or posing a mathematical problem.

  • Start with an authentic problem or question.
  • Give students a real reason to read, write, investigate, and communicate.
  • Return to the problem throughout the unit.

2. Collaborative Reasoning and Discussion

Use when: students need to compare interpretations, critique evidence, explain reasoning, or build shared understanding.

  • Use accountable talk stems and discipline-specific norms.
  • Require evidence, not just opinions.
  • Teach students how to agree, challenge, clarify, and extend.

3. Discipline-Specific Reading and Viewing

Use when: students work with textbooks, primary sources, diagrams, maps, graphs, lab data, technical manuals, videos, simulations, or media.

  • Model how experts approach the text or data.
  • Teach text features, structures, symbols, and representations.
  • Use annotation and sense-making routines.

4. Discipline-Specific Writing

Use when: students explain a process, justify a solution, construct an argument, write a lab report, develop a proof, or produce a product.

  • Study models of writing from the discipline.
  • Teach claim-evidence-reasoning or other field-specific structures.
  • Use short, frequent writing before long formal writing.

5. Academic and Discipline-Specific Language

Use when: students need to interpret technical vocabulary, symbols, word parts, sentence structures, or language that has different meanings across disciplines.

  • Teach vocabulary in context, not as isolated lists.
  • Compare everyday meanings with disciplinary meanings.
  • Support students in using precise language in talk and writing.

6. Culturally Sustaining and Responsive Literacy

Use when: selecting texts, designing problems, building relevance, or helping students see themselves as capable members of the discipline.

  • Use diverse texts, examples, voices, and applications.
  • Connect content to students’ communities and lived experiences.
  • Invite student voice, choice, and identity development.

7. Evidence Evaluation and Source Use

Use when: students research, analyze sources, interpret data, evaluate claims, or make decisions based on information.

  • Teach what counts as evidence in the discipline.
  • Compare source credibility, bias, limitations, and context.
  • Have students justify why evidence supports a claim.

8. Strategy Instruction and Metacognition

Use when: students need support planning, monitoring, revising, and reflecting on reading, writing, problem solving, or discussion.

  • Think aloud as an expert reader/writer/problem solver.
  • Teach students when, why, and how to use strategies.
  • Build reflection into routines, not just end-of-unit tasks.

9. Digital, Multimodal, and Media Literacy

Use when: students use simulations, databases, videos, infographics, maps, models, podcasts, or digital publishing tools.

  • Teach students to analyze how meaning is made across modes.
  • Use digital tools to deepen content knowledge, not decorate work.
  • Discuss credibility, audience, and purpose.

10. Assessment, Feedback, and Transfer

Use when: checking understanding, planning intervention, supporting revision, or helping students apply strategies independently.

  • Use formative checks tied to the literacy demands of the task.
  • Give specific feedback on reasoning, evidence, language, and process.
  • Ask students to transfer strategies to new problems and texts.

Role Dashboards

Use these cards for quick planning by teacher teams, instructional coaches, building administrators, and district leaders.

Classroom Teacher

Primary question: How will students read, write, speak, listen, view, and think like members of this discipline?

  • Identify the unit problem/question.
  • Name the discipline-specific texts and representations.
  • Model expert thinking with think-alouds.
  • Plan discussion, writing, vocabulary, and evidence routines.
  • Use formative assessment to adjust supports.

Building Administrator

Primary question: What conditions help every department implement disciplinary literacy consistently?

  • Build a representative literacy leadership team.
  • Protect collaboration time for departments and cross-content teams.
  • Use multiple data sources, including student voice.
  • Observe for student sense-making, not just teacher coverage.
  • Align resources, intervention, and professional learning.

District Leader

Primary question: How will curriculum, professional learning, resources, and evaluation systems support coherent practice?

  • Adopt a common definition and vision.
  • Align curriculum maps and unit design expectations.
  • Support job-embedded professional learning.
  • Invest in diverse texts, media, databases, and technology.
  • Monitor equity, access, and implementation over time.

8 Essential School-Wide Practices

These practices move disciplinary literacy from isolated classroom strategy use to a coherent building-wide system.

1. Literacy Leadership Team

Create a team with administrators, teacher leaders, department chairs, library/media specialists, coaches, and support staff. Use data and continuous improvement to guide implementation.

2. Collective Responsibility and Safe Climate

Develop a shared belief that all students can grow as disciplinary readers, writers, thinkers, and communicators. Build belonging, agency, and culturally responsive environments.

3. Visible Commitment to Disciplinary Literacy

Make reading, writing, speaking, listening, and viewing visible across classrooms, libraries, media centers, and shared school spaces.

4. Ongoing Professional Learning

Use job-embedded, collaborative, discipline-specific professional learning that includes modeling, observation, feedback, and reflection.

5. Equitable Academic Support

Provide supports in addition to strong core instruction. Differentiate supports based on student strengths and needs, and monitor progress.

6. Response to Student Needs

Coordinate academic, social-emotional, physical, and behavioral supports that may affect disciplinary literacy development.

7. High-Quality Resources

Ensure access to diverse texts, media, digital tools, curricular resources, libraries, and technology that support literacy in all disciplines.

8. Community Networking

Partner with families, local organizations, businesses, libraries, and community groups so students use disciplinary literacy beyond school assignments.

Subject-Specific Disciplinary Literacy Strategies

These examples highlight how disciplinary literacy looks different across content areas. Use them to support department conversations and classroom planning.

Science

  • Interpret graphs, models, and data tables as primary texts.
  • Use Claim-Evidence-Reasoning (CER) for explanations.
  • Engage in modeling and revising scientific explanations.
  • Analyze experimental design and sources of error.

Mathematics

  • Justify solutions using multiple representations.
  • Read and analyze worked examples and proofs.
  • Engage in mathematical discourse comparing strategies.
  • Write explanations of reasoning, not just answers.

Social Studies

  • Source, contextualize, and corroborate documents.
  • Analyze bias, perspective, and reliability.
  • Construct arguments using historical evidence.
  • Engage in civic reasoning and discussion.

ELA

  • Analyze author’s craft, structure, and purpose.
  • Support claims with textual evidence.
  • Write for multiple audiences and purposes.
  • Engage in discussion grounded in interpretation.

CTE

  • Read technical texts (manuals, schematics, procedures).
  • Write for workplace purposes (reports, recommendations).
  • Use discipline-specific vocabulary and tools.
  • Apply literacy to real-world problem solving.

Arts & Electives

  • Critique using discipline-specific language.
  • Interpret visual, musical, or performance texts.
  • Write artist statements and reflections.
  • Analyze technique, style, and audience impact.

Planning Tools

Use these prompts during PLCs, unit planning, walkthrough calibration, coaching cycles, or district curriculum review.

Unit Planning Protocol

  1. Anchor: What authentic problem, phenomenon, question, issue, or challenge drives the unit?
  2. Texts: What texts, data, media, models, visuals, or tools do experts use?
  3. Discourse: How will students talk, listen, debate, explain, question, and critique?
  4. Writing/Product: What will students produce, and for what purpose/audience?
  5. Language: What vocabulary, symbols, sentence structures, or conventions need explicit support?
  6. Assessment: What evidence will show growth in both content and disciplinary literacy?

PLC Conversation Stems

  • What disciplinary thinking are we asking students to do?
  • Where are students reading or viewing something authentic to the discipline?
  • What literacy demand might create a barrier to content learning?
  • How will we model expert thinking before expecting independence?
  • How will student voice, culture, and community knowledge enter the unit?
  • What common evidence will we bring back to the next PLC?
Implementation reminder: Disciplinary literacy should be embedded into the unit and daily content work. It is strongest when students use literacy practices to investigate meaningful questions, build knowledge, and communicate to authentic audiences.

Common Language Glossary

Disciplinary Literacy

Reading, writing, thinking, speaking, listening, and viewing in ways that are specific to a discipline or field.

Authentic Text

Any text, data set, model, visual, media source, tool, or representation used by people in the field.

Evidence-Based Reasoning

Using evidence from texts, data, observations, models, or sources to support a claim, explanation, solution, or interpretation.

Scaffold

A temporary support that helps students access complex disciplinary thinking until they can perform more independently.

Transfer

Students applying a literacy strategy in a new text, problem, task, or content situation without being directly told what to do.

Communication Toolkit

Use these short explanations with staff, families, school boards, or community partners.

Staff Message

Disciplinary literacy is not a separate program. It is the way students learn how to use reading, writing, speaking, listening, viewing, and evidence-based reasoning to think deeply in every subject area.

Family Message

In every class, students are learning how experts in that subject use information. They may analyze data in science, justify reasoning in math, evaluate sources in social studies, or critique creative work in the arts.

School Board / District Message

Disciplinary literacy supports achievement by helping students access complex content, communicate reasoning, evaluate evidence, and apply knowledge across courses, careers, and civic life.

Equity Check

Before launching a strategy or unit, ask:

  • Whose voices, cultures, communities, and experiences are represented?
  • What background knowledge is assumed?
  • Which students may need language, visual, discussion, or vocabulary supports?
  • How will students show understanding in more than one way?
  • How will student voice shape the task, question, or product?
